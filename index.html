Podcast Episode Titles with Strategic Rationale
Business / Entrepreneurship niche

Each title below was written with a specific psychological lever — not just keyword optimization. Good titles don't just describe the episode; they create a felt need in the listener before they press play.

Stop Polishing, Start Profiting: Identifying Your High-ROI Activities
Lever: Behavioral reframe
Targets the costly pattern of mistaking activity for progress. Reframing "polishing" as a low-leverage habit creates urgency to reallocate effort. The promise is direct: shift your focus, shift your results.
The "Unqualified" Advantage: Using Your Imposter Syndrome to Scale
Lever: Cognitive reappraisal
Positions discomfort as a growth indicator, reducing listener resistance. Reframes imposter syndrome as a performance signal — not a limitation. Emotionally validating and action-orienting simultaneously.
How "Invisible Bias" is Costing You Clients
Lever: Loss aversion
Introduces a hidden variable that directly impacts revenue. Loss aversion is one of the strongest behavioral motivators — we act harder to avoid loss than to gain the same value. The title implies a blind spot that must be corrected now.
Stop Tweaking Your Website. Do These 3 Things Instead.
Lever: Pattern interruption + specificity
Addresses a high-frequency inefficiency in plain language. The commanding "Stop" creates pattern interruption. The "3 things" promise reduces cognitive load and signals a structured, actionable episode.

Short-form Script — Wellness / Mental Health
TikTok / Instagram Reels · 30 sec
Hook (0–3s)
"Your body decides you're safe — or in danger — before your alarm even goes off."
Insight (3–20s)
That first 10 minutes after waking? Your cortisol is naturally spiking — it's your body's built-in alarm. But if you grab your phone immediately, you're telling that stress response: yep, there's something to worry about. So it keeps going.
Takeaway / CTA (20–30s)
Give yourself 10 minutes screen-free after waking. Just 10. Let cortisol do its job — then settle. Your nervous system will thank you.
Visual Direction (B-Roll Notes)
Alarm going off → hand reaching for phone (pause mid-reach) → cut to person lying still in soft morning light → close-up of hands around a warm mug. No talking head required.
Psychology applied: The hook creates a knowledge gap ("before your alarm?") and frames the body as an active agent — triggering curiosity without alarm. Principle used: pattern interruption + intrinsic relevance. The CTA is low-friction by design ("just 10") to maximize follow-through.

Your Brain Wasn't Built for This Much Information

Podcast Script Long-Form
"You're Not Crazy" — Dismantling the Stigma Around Mental Disorders
Podcast · Conversational Solo or Guest Format · 35–50 min

A full episode framework — including host script, guest prompts, clip-ready moments, and chapter markers — for a podcast episode tackling the cultural and personal stigma around mental disorders.

Opening Hook / Topic Framing (0:00–3:00)
[Host, conversational] "I want to start with a question I've been sitting with. Have you ever hesitated to tell someone — anyone — that you were seeing a therapist? Or that you'd been diagnosed with something? There's this pause. This calculation. 'How will they see me?' And that pause? That's stigma doing exactly what it was built to do."

Frame: This episode isn't about mental health facts. It's about why knowing the facts isn't always enough to overcome the shame.
Key Segments
1

What Stigma Actually Does (3:00–12:00)

Not just "people judge you." Stigma delays help-seeking by an average of 11 years for mental health conditions. It changes how people describe their symptoms (physical complaints instead of emotional ones). It keeps people from disclosing to employers, family, even doctors. Self-stigma: internalizing shame until you believe you're the problem, not the illness.

Clip moment: "People wait an average of 11 years before seeking help. Stigma is not a feelings issue — it's a public health crisis."
2

Where It Comes From (12:00–22:00)

Language (the words we grew up with — "sira ulo," "crazy," "malabo ang utak"). Media portrayals of mental illness. Religious and cultural frames that pathologize vulnerability. The family system: "we don't talk about that." Guest prompt if applicable: "What was the first message you received — from family, school, religion — about mental health?"

Clip moment: Language segment — high share/save potential.
3

The Disorders Nobody Talks About (22:00–33:00)

OCD beyond the "I like things clean" joke. ADHD in women — missed diagnoses, late diagnoses. Borderline Personality Disorder — the most misunderstood label in psychiatry. Cyclothymia and mood disorders that don't fit the bipolar "template." Guest prompt: "What's the most damaging misconception you've encountered about your diagnosis?"

Clip moment: Each disorder gets its own 60–90 second explainer — 3–4 standalone clips.
4

What Actually Helps (33:00–43:00)

Contact-based destigmatization (hearing personal stories > receiving facts). Language shifts in everyday conversation. How to respond when someone discloses to you. What allies actually do vs. what they think they do. Practical: how to find a therapist in the Philippines, what to expect in a first session.

✦

Close (43:00–50:00)

[Host] "The goal of today wasn't to make you feel sorry for people with mental disorders. It was to make you feel closer to your own humanity — because stigma doesn't just hurt the people with diagnoses. It makes all of us less honest, less connected, less well." CTA: share with one person who needs this.

Clip moment: Full closing — designed as a short-form standalone.

This is how I approach every piece of content — not as a one-off writing task, but as part of a system that maximizes output quality, consistency, and reach without burning out the creator or the audience.

Step 01

Research & Angle Mining

Before I write a word, I find the tension in the topic. What does the audience already believe? What does the data say that contradicts it? The gap between those two things is where the best hooks live. I use Reddit, TikTok comments, YouTube top comments, and Google's "People Also Ask" to find the exact language real people use when they're confused, frustrated, or curious about a topic.

Reddit / Quora AnswerThePublic YouTube Comments TikTok Search
Step 02

Hook-First Architecture

I write the hook last — after I know exactly what the payoff is. A hook only works if it makes a promise the rest of the script can keep. I write 3–5 hook variations per topic, testing different psychological levers: curiosity gap, identity threat, loss aversion, counter-intuitive stat, or personal narrative. Then I pick the one that creates the most tension in the fewest words.

Step 03

Structure for Retention

Short-form: Hook → Insight → Takeaway/CTA. Every second of the script earns its place. Long-form: I map the episode as a narrative arc — not a list of talking points. Where does the tension build? Where does the listener feel seen? Where is the "aha" moment that gets clipped? I flag clip moments during the writing phase, not in post.

Step 04

Visual Thinking from the Start

I write B-roll notes and visual direction into every script draft — not as an afterthought. A script that doesn't consider how it looks on screen is half-finished. I think about what the viewer sees in the first 2 seconds before they hear anything, what text overlays reinforce the spoken hook, and which moments are worth pausing on visually.

Step 05

SEO-Conscious Language

Every health & wellness script is written with searchability in mind — not keyword stuffing, but natural integration of the exact phrases people type into TikTok search, YouTube, and Google. For long-form, I write descriptions and chapter markers that reinforce discoverability. For short-form, I identify hashtag clusters that are niche enough to rank but large enough to matter.

Step 06

Repurpose by Design

I write content systems, not individual scripts. A single long-form episode framework generates: 3–5 short-form clips, a quote card, show notes, a LinkedIn post, and 2–3 Reel captions. This isn't done in post — it's planned at the writing stage. Every piece I write comes with a repurposing map so clients get more mileage from every recording session.
